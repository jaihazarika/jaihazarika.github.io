50 Cognitive Biases

Most of us sleepwalk through life with these biases built in. They condition our thoughts and we don't realize it.

  1. Action bias:
    • The tendency to take action rather than waiting for a better opportunity or more information.
    • Example: A student might choose to answer a question quickly, even if they're not sure of the answer, rather than taking time to think and risk looking slow.
  2. Anchoring bias:
    • The tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered when making decisions.
    • Example: If a store advertises a sale price for a toy, a child might think that's the normal price, even if they see it for less somewhere else.
  3. Availability bias:
    • The tendency to make decisions based on the most easily accessible or memorable information, rather than the complete set of relevant information.
    • Example: If a child hears about a scary news story, they might become afraid of something similar happening to them, even if the odds are very low.
  4. Blind spot bias:
    • The tendency to see oneself as being less biased or less susceptible to biases than others.
    • Example: A child might think they're not biased against someone because of their appearance, but then make assumptions based on stereotypes.
  5. Choice-supportive bias:
    • The tendency to remember one's choices as better than they actually were.
    • Example: A child might remember a toy they chose as being better than it actually was, to justify their decision.
  6. Confirmation bias:
    • The tendency to search, select, analyze, interpret, and recall information in a way that confirms one's preexisting beliefs or hypotheses.
    • Example: A child might only pay attention to information that supports their belief that a certain animal is scary, and ignore information that contradicts it.
  7. Conservatism bias:
    • The tendency to underestimate the likelihood of future events that are different from those that have occurred in the past.
    • Example: A child might think it's unlikely to snow in their town because it hasn't happened before, even if weather patterns are changing.
  8. Conjunction fallacy:
    • The tendency to assume that specific conditions are more probable than general ones.
    • Example: A child might think it's more likely that their friend has a red bike and a helmet, than just a red bike.
  9. Curse of knowledge:
    • The tendency to assume that others have the same knowledge and understanding as oneself.
    • Example: A child might assume that their friend knows what a "baseball glove" is, because they do.
  10. Decision fatigue:
    • The tendency to make worse decisions at the end of the day due to mental exhaustion.
    • Example: A child might choose a less healthy snack in the evening, because they're too tired to make a better choice.
  11. Declinism:
    • The tendency to believe that things were better in the past and will be worse in the future.
    • Example: A child might think that old cartoons were better than the ones made today.
  12. Dunning-Kruger effect:
    • The tendency for individuals who are incompetent in a particular domain to overestimate their own competence and the competence of others who are equally or more competent than themselves.
    • Example: A child who is new to a game might think they're just as good as the more experienced players.
  13. Empathy gap:
    • The tendency to underestimate the influence or strength of feelings, in oneself or others.
    • Example: A child might not realize how scared they would be in a dark room, until they're actually in one.
  14. Endowment effect:
    • The tendency to value something more highly simply because one owns it.
    • Example: A child might not want to trade their favorite toy, even if they're offered something they like just as much.
  15. Experimenter's bias:
    • The tendency for researchers to unconsciously influence the results of their experiments.
    • Example: A child might accidentally give more attention to a plant they're watering, causing it to grow more than the other plants.
  16. Familiarity bias:
    • The tendency to prefer things that are familiar over things that are unfamiliar.
    • Example: A child might choose to read the same book over and over, instead of trying a new one.
  17. Fundamental attribution error:
    • The tendency to attribute other people's behavior to their character rather than their situation.
    • Example: A child might think their friend is mean, instead of realizing they're just having a bad day.
  18. Gambler's fallacy:
    • The tendency to believe that past events will influence future ones, such as believing that a coin toss is "due" for a certain outcome after a series of opposite outcomes.
    • Example: A child might think that if they flip a coin and it lands on heads five times in a row, it's more likely to land on tails the next time.
  19. Halo effect:
    • The tendency to make broad generalizations based on one trait or characteristic.
    • Example: A child might assume that because their teacher is nice, they're also good at their job.
  20. Hindsight bias:
    • The tendency to believe, after an event has occurred, that one would have predicted or expected the outcome beforehand.
    • Example: A child might think they knew all along that their team would win the game, even if they were nervous beforehand.
  21. Illusion of control:
    • The tendency to believe that one has more control over events than is actually the case.
    • Example: A child might think they can make their favorite team win by wearing their lucky shirt.
  22. Illusion of validity:
    • The tendency to overestimate the accuracy of one's predictions based on insufficient evidence.
    • Example: A child might think they can predict which team will win a game, based on their initial impressions of the players.
  23. Illusory superiority:
    • The tendency to overestimate one's abilities relative to others.
    • Example: A child might think they're the best artist in their class, even if they haven't seen everyone else's artwork.
  24. Information bias:
    • The tendency to seek out information even when it is not useful or necessary.
    • Example: A child might spend too much time researching a topic, instead of starting their assignment.
  25. Irrational escalation:
    • The tendency to continue investing in a decision, even when it is clear that it is not the best option.
    • Example: A child might keep trying to build a tower with blocks, even though it keeps falling over.
  26. Just-world hypothesis:
    • The tendency to believe that the world is fundamentally fair, and that people get what they deserve.
    • Example: A child might think that a person who is poor must have done something wrong to deserve it.
  27. Loss aversion:
    • The tendency to prefer avoiding losses to acquiring equivalent gains.
    • Example: A child might be more upset about losing a toy than happy about getting a new one.
  28. Mere exposure effect:
    • The tendency to prefer things that are familiar, even if one cannot consciously remember having encountered them before.
    • Example: A child might prefer a song they've heard before, even if they don't remember where they heard it.
  29. Negativity bias:
    • The tendency to give more weight to negative experiences than positive ones.
    • Example: A child might remember a time they were scolded more than a time they were praised.
  30. Normalcy bias:
    • The tendency to underestimate the likelihood of a disaster or emergency, and to assume that things will continue as they are.
    • Example: A child might not take a fire drill seriously, because they've never experienced a real fire.
  31. Optimism bias:
    • The tendency to overestimate the probability of positive outcomes and underestimate the probability of negative outcomes.
    • Example: A child might think they'll get a perfect score on a test, even if they didn't study.
  32. Overconfidence bias:
    • The tendency to be more confident in one's abilities or predictions than is warranted.
    • Example: A child might think they can ride a bike without training wheels, even if they're not ready yet.
  33. Planning fallacy:
    • The tendency to underestimate how long it will take to complete a task.
    • Example: A child might think they can build a model airplane in an hour, but it takes them three hours.
  34. Projection bias:
    • The tendency to assume that others share the same beliefs, values, and preferences as oneself.
    • Example: A child might assume that their friend likes the same flavor of ice cream as they do.
  35. Recency bias:
    • The tendency to give more weight to recent events or information, and to underestimate the importance of earlier events.
    • Example: A child might think their most recent test score is more important than their earlier ones.
  36. Regression fallacy:
    • The tendency to assume that a single event is part of a larger pattern, rather than being an isolated occurrence.
    • Example: A child might think they're good at basketball because they made a few shots in a row, even if they're not actually good at it.
  37. Restraint bias:
    • The tendency to overestimate one's ability to resist temptation or make good decisions in the future.
    • Example: A child might think they can resist eating candy before dinner, but then give in to the temptation.
  38. Salience bias:
    • The tendency to focus on the most prominent or memorable features of a situation, rather than the most relevant ones.
    • Example: A child might focus on the color of a toy, rather than its educational value.
  39. Self-serving bias:
    • The tendency to take credit for successes and blame external factors for failures.
    • Example: A child might take credit for getting a good grade on a test, but blame the teacher for getting a bad grade.
  40. Sunk cost fallacy:
    • The tendency to continue investing in a decision because of the resources already committed, rather than making a rational decision based on the current situation.
    • Example: A child might continue playing a game they're not enjoying, because they've already spent a lot of time on it.
  41. Survivorship bias:
    • The tendency to focus on the successful outcomes, and to ignore the failures or less successful outcomes.
    • Example: A child might only hear about successful athletes, and think that's the only outcome of playing sports.
  42. Temporal discounting:
    • The tendency to value immediate rewards more highly than future rewards.
    • Example: A child might choose to eat a candy bar now, instead of saving it for later.
  43. Thought-action fusion:
    • The tendency to believe that thinking about a negative event is morally equivalent to causing it to happen.
    • Example: A child might think that thinking about a bad grade will make it happen.
  44. Zero-risk bias:
    • The tendency to prefer options that eliminate all risk, even if it means accepting a lower overall benefit.
    • Example: A child might choose to stay home instead of going on a trip, because they're afraid of getting lost.
  45. Anchoring and adjustment heuristic:
    • The tendency to make estimates based on the first piece of information encountered, and then adjust based on additional information.
    • Example: A child might estimate that a group of 20 animals is more than 50, because they initially thought there were more than 50.
  46. Availability heuristic:
    • The tendency to make judgments about the likelihood of events based on how easily they can be brought to mind.
    • Example: A child might think that getting lost in a store is more likely than getting lost in a park, because they can easily remember times they got lost in a store.
  47. Representativeness heuristic:
    • The tendency to make judgments about the probability of an event based on how closely it resembles a prototype or stereotype.
    • Example: A child might think that a person with glasses is smart, because that's what they've seen in movies and TV shows.
  48. Simulation heuristic:
    • The tendency to make decisions based on mental simulations of possible outcomes, rather than on actual data or evidence.
    • Example: A child might decide not to ride a bike because they imagine themselves falling off, even if they've never fallen off before.
  49. Substitution heuristic:
    • The tendency to substitute a difficult question with an easier one when making a decision.
    • Example: A child might choose a flavor of ice cream based on the color, instead of their actual preference.
  50. Tallying heuristic:
    • The tendency to make decisions based on a mental tally of positive and negative experiences or outcomes.
    • Example: A child might choose a restaurant based on the number of times they've had a good meal there, rather than the quality of the food.
